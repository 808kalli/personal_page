diploma thesis

Teacher-Student Distillation for Efficient Deployment of VLA Models in Robotic Manipulation

Elias Kallioras · NTUA School of ECE · Feb 2026
Supervised by Prof. Panayiotis Tsanakas (NTUA) and Dr. Vasileios Balntas (University of Oxford)

Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models have demonstrated strong generalisation to novel manipulation tasks by grounding language instructions in visual observations. However, their size makes real-time deployment on robotic hardware impractical. This thesis investigates whether teacher-student knowledge distillation can compress VLA models while preserving task performance.

I designed a distillation framework where a large, pre-trained VLA teacher supervises a smaller student model through a combination of behaviour cloning on demonstration data and intermediate feature-level distillation. Experiments were conducted on the LIBERO benchmark suite, covering a range of manipulation tasks with varying object diversity and instruction complexity.

The results show that the student model achieves competitive task success rates at a fraction of the inference cost, with LoRA-based fine-tuning enabling efficient adaptation. The work also identifies key failure modes — particularly in long-horizon tasks — that remain open challenges for distilled policies.

All Interactions Matter: Modality-preserving Deep Fusion for Multimodal Sentiment Analysis

Elias Kallioras, Nikolos Xiros, et al. · Interspeech 2026 (in submission)

Multimodal sentiment analysis aims to infer sentiment from combinations of text, audio, and visual signals. Most existing fusion approaches lose modality-specific information in the process of cross-modal interaction, particularly when one modality dominates or when cross-modal attention is applied naively.

This work proposes a modality-preserving deep fusion architecture that explicitly maintains each modality's representation throughout the fusion process. By ensuring that individual modalities are not collapsed early, the model retains fine-grained signals from each input stream, which improves performance on ambiguous or noisy samples where all available cues must be leveraged.

The method is evaluated on standard multimodal sentiment benchmarks and shows consistent improvements over fusion baselines, especially on instances where cross-modal cues are complementary rather than redundant.
