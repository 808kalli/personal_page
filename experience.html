Robotics & AI Engineering Intern

I-SENSE Research Group — Athens, Greece
Mar – Aug 2025

At I-SENSE I worked on an interactive 3D semantic mapping system that combines open-vocabulary vision models with classical LiDAR SLAM. The goal was to let a user query a reconstructed 3D environment using natural language — for example, "show me all chairs" — and get back color-coded 3D regions matching the description.

The pipeline runs CLIP and SAM on RGB images to generate segmentation masks with semantic embeddings, which are then associated with the 3D point cloud produced by the SLAM backend. Query-time retrieval uses cosine similarity between the query embedding and stored segment embeddings to rank and highlight regions in the 3D scene viewer.

This project gave me hands-on experience integrating large vision models into real-time robotic pipelines, dealing with latency constraints, and working with ROS and the full sensor stack (LiDAR + RGB-D camera).

Diploma Thesis Researcher

NTUA — School of ECE
2025 – 2026 · Supervised by Prof. P. Tsanakas (NTUA) and Dr. V. Balntas (Oxford)

My diploma thesis focuses on making Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models practical for deployment on robotic hardware. These models are powerful generalists — they can follow language instructions across novel manipulation tasks — but their inference cost is prohibitive for real-time control.

I designed a teacher-student distillation framework where a compact student model learns from a large pre-trained VLA teacher through a combination of behavioural cloning on demonstration data and feature-level distillation at selected intermediate layers. LoRA-based fine-tuning keeps the adaptation computationally tractable.

Experiments on the LIBERO benchmark show that the student achieves competitive task success rates at significantly reduced inference cost. The work also surfaces interesting failure modes in distilled policies on long-horizon tasks, which I discuss in the thesis as directions for future work.

IEEE NTUA Robotics and Automation Society (RAS)

Member / Project Coordinator
Mar 2022 – Jun 2024

I joined IEEE RAS NTUA in my second year and eventually coordinated the Autonomous Driving Vehicle (ADV) project. The project's goal was to build a small-scale autonomous car capable of lane following, intersection handling, and traffic sign compliance.

My main technical contribution was implementing a real-time traffic sign detection and classification pipeline using YOLOv5, trained on a custom dataset we collected and annotated. The pipeline ran onboard and fed decisions into the vehicle's control stack via ROS topics.

I also contributed to RAS Academy, a semester-long robotics workshop series we ran for undergraduate students — writing tutorials, preparing hands-on exercises, and co-presenting sessions on computer vision and ROS fundamentals.

Bosch Future Mobility Challenge

Competitor — Autonomous Driving
Nov 2022 – Jun 2024

The Bosch Future Mobility Challenge is an annual university competition where teams build autonomous vehicles and compete on a standardised miniature track. I competed twice, in 2023 and 2024, as part of the NTUA team.

My contributions spanned the full stack: control algorithms (PID and model-based lateral control), sensor fusion for localization using a combination of camera odometry and IMU, and a deep learning pipeline for traffic sign recognition. In 2024 I took a more senior role, helping newer team members integrate their modules and debugging the full system under time pressure at the competition venue.

The competition is a demanding environment — live hardware, real track conditions, time-boxed debugging — and a good forcing function for writing robust, testable code.

PROM Racing NTUA — Formula Student Driverless

Software Member
Nov 2022 – May 2023

PROM Racing is NTUA's Formula Student team. I joined the driverless division and worked on the perception and mapping pipeline for autonomous lap completion on an unknown circuit marked by coloured cones.

The key challenge is that the vehicle has no prior map — it must detect cones with a camera and LiDAR, triangulate their positions, and build a local map on the fly while simultaneously estimating its own pose. I contributed to the SLAM backend, written in C for performance, focusing on the map update and loop closure components.

Working in a Formula Student team was a good introduction to writing low-level, performance-critical code under strict latency budgets, and to integrating software with real hardware in a competitive setting.
